# S067: Phillips Curve, US, 1955-2010

**Chapter 12: The Rise and Fall of the Phillips Curve** | **Figures**: Fig12.8 | **Period**: 1955–2010

## What This Measures

The conventional Phillips curve relationship between unemployment and inflation for the US, 1955–2010. Components include IMF credit data (credit to the domestic economy per capita), the official unemployment rate, and CPI inflation.

Shaikh devotes Chapter 12 to demonstrating that the Phillips curve — the supposed inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation — is empirically unstable and theoretically unfounded. The data shows that the curve "rose and fell" as a reliable empirical regularity: it appeared to hold in the 1960s, broke down spectacularly during the stagflation of the 1970s, and has been unreliable since. Shaikh argues that the classical theory of wages and unemployment (Chapter 14) provides a better framework, in which the real wage is determined by the balance of power between labor and capital, not by a mechanical trade-off with inflation.
