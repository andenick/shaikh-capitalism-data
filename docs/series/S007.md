# S007: Manufacturing Productivity and Real Wages

**Chapter 2: Turbulent Trends and Hidden Structures** | **Figures**: Fig2.5 | **Period**: 1889–2025

## What This Measures

Indexes of manufacturing productivity (output per worker-hour) and real employee compensation, both rebased to 1889=100. Productivity is spliced from BEA Long Term Economic Growth (1966, Series A173) for 1860–1970 and BLS International Labor Comparisons for 1950 onward. Real compensation uses CPI-deflated production worker wages from the same sources.

Shaikh emphasizes that "productivity growth is essentially a measure of technical change" driven by profit-seeking competition (Chapter 7), and that "one of the great strengths of developed capitalism is that real wages also generally rise over the long run." However, the series makes visible the dramatic divergence that began in the early 1980s: "beginning with the Reagan-led assault on labor and compounded by foreign competition, US manufacturing workers suffered a remarkable stagnation in real wages, one that continues into the present." Shaikh's core point is that "productivity growth provides the material foundation for a potential rise in real wages... But productivity growth does not automatically lead to growth in real wages. It takes social and institutional mechanisms to create (often hard won) linkages between the two, and these connections can always be rent asunder."

## From the Book

> Manufacturing productivity (yr) for 1860-1970 (1958 = 100) from BEA (1966, Series A173) and for 1950-2009 from BLS International Data, Table 1: Output per Hour in Manufacturing, 19 Countries or Areas (2007 = 100). Both series rebased to 1889 = 100 and spliced in 1950.
>
> — Shaikh (2016), Appendix 2.1, Figure 2.5
