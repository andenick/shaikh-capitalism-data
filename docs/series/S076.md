# S076: Consumer Price Level, US, 1774-2011

**Chapter 15: Modern Money and Inflation** | **Figures**: Fig15.1 | **Period**: 1774–2011

## What This Measures

The US Consumer Price Index from 1774 to 2011, sourced from MeasuringWorth.com. This is one of the longest continuous price series available for any country.

Shaikh uses this series to make a fundamental point about the nature of inflation: that persistent price increases are a specifically modern phenomenon. In historical perspective (Figure 15.1), the price level was essentially stationary for over 150 years — rising and falling with wars and commodity cycles but showing no secular trend. Persistent inflation only begins after the abandonment of commodity-money anchors in the 20th century. Shaikh argues that modern inflation is driven by the interaction of credit creation, profitability, and labor costs — not by the quantity of money as monetarist theory claims.
