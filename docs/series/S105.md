# S105: Corporate Incremental Rate of Profit (IROP)

**Chapter 6: Capital and Profit** | **Figures**: Fig6.7 | **Period**: 1948–2024

## What This Measures

The incremental rate of profit (IROP) measures the return on new investment: irop = ΔGOS / (IG + ΔINV) - 1, where Δ is the first difference, GOS is gross operating surplus, IG is gross fixed investment, and INV is inventories. The nominal IROP averages ~13–14% and the real IROP averages ~8–10% over 1947–2007.

Shaikh emphasizes that the IROP captures information distinct from the average profit rate: "the incremental rate of profit measures the return on new investment. Corrected and NIPA incremental rates track very closely, indicating that corrections matter more for levels than for changes." The IROP is inherently more volatile than the average rate (being a first-difference measure), but its trend follows the same secular decline that characterizes the average profit rate — confirming that new investment faces the same falling-profitability dynamic that governs the stock of capital.
